## Why Most Video Projects Fail (Before Filming Even Starts)

The video production industry has a dirty secret: most projects are doomed before the first shot is framed.

Not because of bad cinematography or poor editing. Because nobody took the time to understand what the video actually needed to accomplish.

Think about it from your perspective as a business leader. You know video marketing works—93% of marketers report positive ROI. But you're also nervous. Will this video company understand the technical complexity of what you do? Will they "get" the nuances of your industry? Can they tell the difference between what you think you need and what will actually solve your business problem?

These aren't irrational fears. They're pattern recognition from watching too many agencies produce beautiful work that doesn't move the needle.

The gap between "pretty video" and "effective video" is closed during discovery. Here's what goes wrong when companies skip it:

**The Assumption Trap:** The client says "recruitment video" and the agency assumes they know what that means. In manufacturing, a recruitment video for welders looks completely different than one for engineers. For agriculture operations, seasonal timing changes everything. For energy companies, regulatory compliance isn't optional—it's existential.

**The Feature Fallacy:** Clients often lead with what they want to show (new equipment, facility tour, leadership interviews) rather than what they need to achieve (reduce time-to-hire by 30%, increase qualified applicants, change perception in the community). Features are easy to discuss. Outcomes require deeper conversation.

**The Alignment Failure:** Marketing wants brand awareness. Operations wants safety compliance. HR wants recruitment. Finance wants measurable ROI. Without discovering who the true stakeholders are and what success looks like to each of them, you'll produce a video that pleases no one.

Research shows that fixing problems discovered at the planning stage costs 10-100 times less than fixing them during production or after delivery. That manufacturing project that cost us three weeks? In financial terms, we spent $8,000 fixing a problem that a 90-minute discovery call would have prevented.

The math is brutal when you skip discovery. But it's transformative when you invest in it properly.

## The Pre-Production Alignment Framework: How We Actually Run Discovery

Our discovery process isn't a sales pitch disguised as a consultation. It's a structured investigation designed to uncover three things:

1. **What story actually exists** (not what you think exists)
2. **What business outcome you're trying to create** (not what type of video you think you need)
3. **What success looks like in measurable terms** (not vague aspirations)

We've adapted a methodology called the Pre-Production Alignment Framework (PAF), refining it over hundreds of projects. Here's what happens:

### Phase 1: Situational Understanding (15-20 minutes)

We're not asking about your video yet. We're asking about your business.

- What's happening in your industry right now that's creating pressure or opportunity?
- What's your current challenge that made video seem like the right solution?
- What have you already tried that didn't work?

For a Lethbridge-based manufacturing client last year, this phase uncovered that their "recruitment video" request was actually a retention problem. Their seasoned welders were leaving for competitors, and they assumed they needed to attract new talent. The real issue? Current employees didn't feel valued or understood. The video strategy shifted completely—we created a "day in the life" series that showcased the craftsmanship and expertise of current welders, which they shared proudly on social media. Turnover dropped 40% in six months.

We would have missed that with a standard intake form.

### Phase 2: Problem Excavation (20-25 minutes)

This is where we use SPIN Selling techniques—Situation, Problem, Implication, Need-Payoff questions—to go deeper.

**Situation questions** establish context: "Walk me through what happens when a potential hire first hears about your company."

**Problem questions** identify pain points: "Where in that process do you lose the candidates you actually want?"

**Implication questions** amplify urgency: "If this continues for another 12 months, what does that cost you in terms of lost production, overtime, or project delays?"

**Need-Payoff questions** build vision: "If we could show potential hires exactly what a career path looks like at your company—the training, advancement, community—how would that change your recruitment conversations?"

Most agencies stop after problem questions. The implication and need-payoff phases are where we discover what the video actually needs to accomplish.

### Phase 3: Story Mining (25-30 minutes)

Here's the secret: your best stories already exist. They're in the experiences of your employees, the challenges you've overcome, the moments when your values were tested.

We ask behavioral questions:

- "Tell me about a time when your team solved a problem everyone said was impossible."
- "What's a customer or employee interaction that reminded you why you're in this business?"
- "What do your best employees say about working here that surprises you?"

For an agriculture client near Brooks, we asked: "What's the biggest misconception city people have about modern farming?" That question led to a 30-minute conversation about precision agriculture, environmental stewardship, and the intersection of tradition and technology. We captured that authentic frustration and pride in the final video, which became their most-shared content ever (14,000 views, 120+ shares in a region with 14,000 residents).

The story was always there. Discovery just gave them permission to tell it.

### Phase 4: Stakeholder Mapping (10-15 minutes)

Who else cares about this video's success? What does success look like to each of them?

We create a simple stakeholder map:

- **Primary audience:** Who needs to watch this and take action?
- **Secondary audiences:** Who else will see it?
- **Internal stakeholders:** Who has approval authority? Who measures results?
- **Success metrics:** How will each stakeholder know this worked?

For a nonprofit client in Lethbridge, we discovered that "donor engagement" meant completely different things to the development director (recurring monthly donors), the program manager (volunteer recruitment), and the board chair (major gift cultivation). We ended up creating a primary video optimized for mid-level donors, with three cut-down versions for the other audiences. Each version emphasized different elements from the same shoot day.

Without stakeholder mapping, we would have created one video that tried to do everything and accomplished nothing.

### Phase 5: Vision Lock and Scope Agreement (15-20 minutes)

The final phase is where we play back what we've heard and confirm alignment.

"Based on what you've shared, here's what I'm hearing: You need a video that shows potential engineering hires that your company offers technical challenges, mentorship from industry veterans, and a path to leadership—not just a job. Success looks like 25% more qualified applicants within 90 days and a 30-second average watch time on LinkedIn. Does that capture it?"

This is where clients often say: "That's _exactly_ it—I didn't even know how to articulate that until you said it."

That moment—when a client feels truly understood—is when trust gets built. And trust is the currency that allows us to do our best creative work.

We document everything in a Discovery Summary document, which becomes the foundation for the creative brief, production plan, and success measurement framework.

## The Questions That Uncover Authentic Stories

Not all questions are created equal. After analyzing hundreds of discovery calls, we've identified the questions that consistently surface the stories worth telling.

### The Unexpected Benefits Question

"What do your employees/customers/community members say about you that surprises you?"

This question bypasses the corporate messaging and gets to authentic perception. For an energy sector client, they were surprised that local landowners praised their "obsessive communication during projects." That became the emotional core of their community relations video.

### The Moment of Truth Question

"Tell me about a time when you had to make a decision that tested your values."

Corporate values are meaningless until they're stress-tested. A manufacturing client told us about turning down a lucrative contract because the client wanted them to cut corners on safety. That story—shared by the shop foreman who spoke up—became the emotional anchor of their recruitment video. Applications from experienced tradespeople increased 65%.

### The Contrarian Question

"What does everyone in your industry do that you refuse to do?"

Differentiation isn't about being better. It's about being different in ways that matter. An agriculture client told us they refuse to use growth hormones, even though it makes their cattle take longer to reach market weight. That commitment to animal welfare and product quality became their brand story.

### The Time Machine Question

"If you could show a new employee/customer/donor what their experience will look like 6 months from now, what would you show them?"

This question forces forward thinking and uncovers aspirational content. A nonprofit client described the moment when a volunteer realizes they've made a tangible difference in someone's life. We built the entire video around capturing that transformation.

### The Third-Party Validation Question

"What would your best customer/employee/partner say about you if we interviewed them right now?"

This surfaces social proof and testimonial opportunities. More importantly, it reveals what _you_ believe others value about you—which is often more authentic than your own marketing copy.

These aren't interrogation techniques. They're invitations to reflection. When asked with genuine curiosity and active listening, they unlock stories that clients didn't know they had permission to tell.

## Active Listening: The Skill That Separates Good Discovery from Great Discovery

Here's a humbling truth: the questions matter less than how you listen to the answers.

Research on consultative selling shows that the ideal talk-to-listen ratio is 35:65. The client should be talking almost twice as much as you are. But listening isn't passive silence—it's active investigation.

### The Three Levels of Listening

**Level 1: Hearing Words** - You're listening for facts, features, specifications. This is where most discovery calls stay.

**Level 2: Understanding Meaning** - You're listening for emotions, priorities, concerns underneath the words. When a client says "we need this fast," are they excited about market opportunity or anxious about competitive pressure? The strategy changes based on the answer.

**Level 3: Sensing What's Unsaid** - You're noticing hesitations, energy shifts, what topics they light up about versus what they rush through. When a manufacturing client mentioned their "excellent safety record" in a flat tone but became animated discussing "the craftsmanship of our machinists," we knew where the authentic story lived.

### Techniques We Use

**Paraphrasing:** "So what I'm hearing is that you've tried recruitment videos before, but they attracted quantity over quality. You'd rather have 10 highly qualified applicants than 100 who don't understand the technical demands. Is that right?"

Paraphrasing serves two purposes: it confirms understanding and it gives clients the chance to refine their thinking. Often they'll respond with: "Yes, exactly—and actually, let me add..."

**Reflecting Emotion:** "It sounds like you're frustrated that people don't understand how sophisticated modern agriculture has become."

Naming the emotion validates it and often unlocks deeper storytelling. That agricultural client responded: "Frustrated? I'm tired of being dismissed as 'just a farmer' when we're running precision equipment, analyzing soil data, and managing environmental systems. We're agronomists, technologists, and business operators." That emotional truth became the video's narrative spine.

**Strategic Silence:** After asking a good question, wait. Count to five before jumping in. The best insights come in the pause after the initial answer.

A nonprofit client, when asked "What moment in your work gives you hope?", initially gave a generic answer about "seeing lives changed." We waited. After seven seconds of silence, she added: "Actually, there was this one kid—came to us angry, closed off, headed down a bad path. Eighteen months later, he's volunteering to help other kids like him. That's when I know this work matters." That story became the video's opening.

### The Diagnostic Mindset

The best discovery calls feel less like interviews and more like collaborative problem-solving.

We're not there to take an order for a video. We're there to diagnose whether video is even the right solution—and if so, what kind of video will actually solve the business problem.

Sometimes that means recommending a different approach. We once had an energy company request a corporate overview video. During discovery, we realized what they actually needed was a crisis communication plan and reputation management strategy. We referred them to a PR firm we trust.

They hired us six months later for a different project, specifically because we'd been honest about what they needed rather than what we wanted to sell.

That's the paradox of great discovery: sometimes it talks you out of a sale in the short term to build trust for the long term.

## From Discovery to Production: How Insights Become Action

The discovery call isn't the end of pre-production—it's the foundation.

Everything that follows builds on the alignment created in that 90-minute conversation:

### Creative Brief Development

We translate discovery insights into a creative brief that includes:

- **Business Objective:** Measurable outcome (not video deliverable)
- **Core Message:** The one thing we want viewers to think, feel, or do
- **Narrative Approach:** Documentary-style? Testimonial-driven? Problem/solution?
- **Emotional Tone:** Pride? Hope? Determination? Trust?
- **Success Metrics:** Views, engagement, leads, applications, donations—whatever matters to stakeholders

The brief is shared with the client for final alignment before any filming decisions are made.

### Production Planning

Discovery insights inform every production decision:

- **Location selection:** Where do the authentic moments happen?
- **Interview subjects:** Who has the credibility and story to carry the message?
- **B-roll priorities:** What visual metaphors support the core message?
- **Scheduling:** When are the meaningful activities happening? (Harvest season for agriculture, shift changes for manufacturing, donor events for nonprofits)

For that manufacturing retention project, discovery revealed that the most meaningful moments happened during shift handoff—when outgoing machinists briefed incoming ones about ongoing projects. We scheduled the shoot specifically to capture those 15-minute exchanges, which became the visual backbone of the final video.

### Risk Mitigation

Discovery also surfaces potential problems:

- Regulatory or compliance restrictions (common in energy sector)
- Union or employee privacy considerations (manufacturing)
- Seasonal or weather dependencies (agriculture)
- Approval chain complexity (nonprofits with boards)

Identifying these early prevents costly delays or re-shoots.
